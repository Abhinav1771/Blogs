### üîç The Paradox Stated Correctly

Let B ‚äÜ ‚Ñù¬≥ be a solid ball (unit sphere). The Banach-Tarski Paradox states:

**There exists a finite decomposition:** B = A‚ÇÅ ‚à™ A‚ÇÇ ‚à™ A‚ÇÉ ‚à™ A‚ÇÑ ‚à™ A‚ÇÖ

**And rotations R‚ÇÅ, R‚ÇÇ, R‚ÇÉ, R‚ÇÑ ‚àà SO(3) such that:**

* R‚ÇÅ(A‚ÇÅ) ‚à™ R‚ÇÇ(A‚ÇÇ) ‚à™ R‚ÇÉ(A‚ÇÉ) = B (first copy)
    
* R‚ÇÑ(A‚ÇÑ) ‚à™ R‚ÇÖ(A‚ÇÖ) = B (second copy)
    

**Key Point:** All five pieces are used to reconstruct two complete balls of the same size as the original.

## üî¨ How the Mathematical Construction Works

### üß± 1. The Free Subgroup Construction

The construction relies on finding a **free subgroup** F‚ÇÇ ‚äÇ SO(3) generated by two rotations a and b, where:

* F‚ÇÇ = ‚ü®a, b‚ü© (free subgroup, not free group)
    
* Elements are finite words like: a, b, ab, ba‚Åª¬π, aba‚Åª¬πb‚Åª¬π, etc.
    
* No relations exist between a and b within this subgroup
    

**Example rotations:**

* a: rotation by arccos(1/3) around x-axis
    
* b: rotation by arccos(1/3) around a carefully chosen second axis
    

### üîÅ 2. Group Action and Orbits

The free subgroup F‚ÇÇ acts on points in the sphere S¬≤ (boundary of the ball). For any point x ‚àà S¬≤, its orbit is:

Orb(x) = {g(x) : g ‚àà F‚ÇÇ}

This partitions the sphere into equivalence classes (orbits).

### üéØ 3. Non-Measurable Sets via Axiom of Choice

Using the Axiom of Choice, we:

1. Select one representative from each orbit ‚Üí creates set H
    
2. Define the pieces as: A‚ÇÅ = aH, A‚ÇÇ = bH, A‚ÇÉ = H \\ {specific points}, etc.
    
3. These pieces are **non-measurable** - they have no well-defined volume
    

**Crucial insight:** The non-measurability is what allows the "impossible" rearrangement.

### üé≤ Axiom of Choice: Picking One Point per Orbit

From each orbit, the Axiom of Choice allows us to pick one representative. This forms a set X ‚äÇ B, which is non-measurable. Pick **1 point per orbit** (using Axiom of Choice) ‚Üí this is Set X.

### ‚öôÔ∏è The Role of the Axiom of Choice

* Allows selection from countless orbits
    
* Enables the construction of non-measurable sets
    

Without it, Banach‚ÄìTarski can‚Äôt be proven.

**Algorithm: Finite Orbit Approximation**

```plaintext
Input: Finite point set P on the unit sphere, rotation generators a, b
Output: Visualisation of orbit dynamics

1. Generate finite word set W = {group elements up to length n}
2. For each point p ‚àà P:
   - Compute approximate orbit: O(p) = {w(p) : w ‚àà W}
   - Track orbit intersections and separations
3. Visualise:
   - Original vs transformed point clouds
   - Orbit relationship networks
   - Group action effects on sphere regions
```

### Key Insights from the Framework

2. **Near-Disjointness**: Different orbits avoid each other in surprising ways
    
3. **Dense Coverage**: Group actions can create dense coverings of the sphere

## Why This Cannot Work in Reality

The paradox faces several physical impossibilities:

1. **Atomic Structure**: Real objects are made of discrete atoms, not continuous matter
    
2. **Quantum Mechanics**: Heisenberg uncertainty principle prevents precise positioning
    
3. **Measurability**: Physical quantities must be measurable
    
4. **Axiom of Choice**: Cannot be applied to physical selection processes

* It reveals deep issues with naive concepts of "measure"
    
* It shows the power and strangeness of infinite mathematics
    
* It's not actually contradictory - just counterintuitive
